The material problem

Ordinary lumber
fights the North.

A standard treated deck greys, cups, and starts to rot after a handful of Northern Minnesota seasons. The reason is in the board itself: ordinary lumber holds moisture and sugars, and freeze-thaw works them like a lever until the wood moves. So you spend every spring sanding, staining, and sealing just to slow it down.

We solve that at the material. Arbor Wood Co. runs real, USA-sourced wood through a three-phase thermal modification process that removes the moisture and sugars driving the rot and the warp — so the board stays flatter, longer, with far less fuss. Here's what goes into a Pro Con deck:

  • Frost-depth footings. Sized and poured below the Northern Minnesota frost line so the deck won't heave when the ground freezes.
  • Framing & structure. The beams, joists, and posts that carry the deck and keep it dead level under load.
  • Arbor Wood thermally modified decking. USA-sourced boards with the moisture and sugars cooked out — stable, slow to grey, no annual reseal demanded.
  • Railings & stairs. Built to code and to the way you move on and off the deck, not an afterthought bolted on at the end.
  • Flashing where the deck meets the house. The ledger detail most failures start at — flashed so water sheds away from your wall, not into it.

Deck questions

Answered.

What is Arbor Wood thermally modified wood, and why use it on a Duluth deck?

Arbor Wood Co. is real wood, USA-sourced, run through a three-phase thermal modification process that drives out the moisture and sugars ordinary lumber holds onto. Those are exactly what make a board cup, swell, and grey out — so on a Duluth deck that sees freeze-thaw and lake-effect damp, it stays flatter and more stable for far longer than standard treated lumber.

How deep do deck footings need to go in Northern Minnesota?

We set footings to frost depth, which in the Duluth area means roughly five feet down. A deck on shallow footings heaves every winter as the ground freezes and lifts; footings poured below the frost line stay put, so your deck stays level for the life of the structure.

How long does it take to build a deck?

Most decks are built in one to three weeks. The span depends on size, the footing dig, stairs, and railing, but we give you a real schedule with the written estimate before any digging starts.

Will a thermally modified deck need staining and sealing every year?

No. Because the moisture and sugars are already cooked out of Arbor Wood, it isn't on the annual stain-and-seal treadmill that ordinary lumber demands. Left alone it weathers to a soft silver-grey; if you want to hold the warmer tone, an occasional oil finish does it — your choice, not a yearly chore.

Do you build in Two Harbors and along the North Shore, not just Duluth?

Yes. We build decks across Northern Minnesota — Duluth, Two Harbors, Hermantown, Cloquet, and the surrounding communities. Pro Con is licensed in Minnesota under #QB807406 and insured, and every deck carries a workmanship guarantee.
